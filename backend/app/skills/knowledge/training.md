# Fencing Training Management Knowledge

## Daily Training Structure

### Warm-Up (15-20 minutes)
1. **General Warm-Up**
   - Light jogging or jumping jacks
   - Dynamic stretching (leg swings, arm circles)
   - Joint mobility exercises (ankles, hips, shoulders)

2. **Fencing-Specific Warm-Up**
   - Footwork drills at low intensity
   - Shadow fencing without opponent
   - Blade work drills: extensions, withdrawals, beats

### Main Training (60-90 minutes)

#### Technical Training (40%)
- Footwork practice: advances, retreats, lung
- Blade workes, recoveries: attacks, parries, ripostes
- Individual lesson with coach or partner drill

#### Tactical Training (30%)
- Situational sparring
- Specific action repetition
- Decision-making drills

#### Physical Training (30%)
- Strength exercises (core, legs, upper body)
- Plyometrics for explosive power
- Flexibility exercises

### Cool-Down (10-15 minutes)
- Light stretching
- Core stabilization exercises
- Mental review of session

## Weekly Training Plan

### Monday - Technical Focus
- Morning: Footwork intensive (60 min)
- Evening: Blade work drills (60 min)

### Tuesday - Physical Training
- Strength training (45 min)
- Cardio/intervals (30 min)
- Light technical review (30 min)

### Wednesday - Mixed Training
- Technical sparring (45 min)
- Tactical situations (45 min)
- Video analysis (30 min)

### Thursday - Active Recovery
- Light footwork (30 min)
- Stretching/yoga (45 min)
- Mental visualization (20 min)

### Friday - Competition Prep
- Full bout simulation (60 min)
- Specific weapon techniques (45 min)
- Strategy review (30 min)

### Saturday - Tournament/Light Training
- Competition or mock bouts (90 min)
- Technique refinement (30 min)

### Sunday - Rest
- Complete rest or light activity
- Nutrition and recovery

## Training Cycle Planning

### Microcycle (1 week)
- High intensity: 2-3 days
- Medium intensity: 2-3 days
- Recovery: 1-2 days

### Mesocycle (3-4 weeks)
- **Week 1**: Volume building (70% intensity, 100% volume)
- **Week 2**: Peak intensity (90% intensity, 80% volume)
- **Week 3**: Competition phase (100% intensity, 60% volume)
- **Week 4**: Recovery and testing

### Macrocycle (3-6 months)
- **Preparation Phase** (6-8 weeks): Base fitness and technique
- **Competition Phase** (8-12 weeks): Tactical refinement
- **Peak Phase** (2-4 weeks): Competition readiness
- **Transition Phase** (1-2 weeks): Active recovery

## Recovery and Rest

### Daily Recovery
- 8-9 hours of sleep
- Proper hydration (2-3L water daily)
- Post-training nutrition within 30 minutes
- Light stretching or foam rolling

### Weekly Recovery
- At least 1-2 rest days per week
- Active recovery: swimming, cycling, walking
- Mental breaks from fencing

### Recovery Indicators
- Morning resting heart rate (track for anomalies)
- Sleep quality
- Muscle soreness (1-10 scale)
- Training motivation

### Overtraining Prevention
- Monitor training load
- Progress gradually (10% rule)
- Include deload weeks every 4-6 weeks

## Training Intensity Management

### Intensity Zones
1. **Zone 1 (50-60%)**: Recovery, technique practice
2. **Zone 2 (60-70%)**: Endurance building, drilling
3. **Zone 3 (70-80%)**: Tactical training, controlled sparring
4. **Zone 4 (80-90%)**: Competition intensity bouts
5. **Zone 5 (90-100%)**: Peak performance, finals

### Monitoring Tools
- Heart rate monitoring
- Rate of Perceived Exertion (RPE) scale
- Training logs
- Coach feedback

### Periodization Principles
- Progressive overload
- Variation in training stimulus
- Specificity based on competition calendar
- Individualization for each fencer

---

## Key Training Principles

### SAID Principle
- Specific Adaptation to Imposed Demands
- Training must be specific to fencing movements

### Overload Principle
- Gradually increase training demands
- Can vary intensity, volume, or complexity

### Reversibility
- Fitness gains lost without consistent training
- Maintain minimum training frequency

### Individual Differences
- Age, experience, and physical attributes matter
- Customize training plans accordingly
